The #1 Thing to Know About California Squirrels

Many are unaware that most tree squirrels seen in California’s cities and suburbs today are not native to the region.

Eastern Grays and Fox Squirrels were introduced in the early 1900s after habitat loss and disease reduced native populations on the West Coast. This site helps you understand the difference, because identifying which squirrel you’re seeing is the first step toward knowing how to interact with it.

An Ancient Lineage

Our Squirrel-Like Ancestors: Survivors of the Asteroid Impact


Squirrel Evolution

Today's squirrels evolved from small, shrew-like mammals that survived the asteroid impact 66 million years ago, which caused the extinction of the dinosaurs. These early mammals avoided extinction by burrowing, being active at night, and feeding on insects, traits that helped them adapt to the harsh environmental conditions that followed and ultimately shaped the evolution of many modern mammals, including humans.





What is a Tree Squirrel?

Tree Squirrels Nest in Trees

Tree squirrels typically build their nests in trees, rather than underground. They are agile climbers, have long bushy tails for balance, and rely on a tree canopy to stay safe from predators.

Close Cousins - NOT Tree Squirrels


Ground Squirrels


Chipmunks

Ground Squirrels and Chipmunks nest underground, have stripes or spots, and sport cheek pouches for food collecting. Tree squirrels do not. In short: If it nests in a tree, it’s likely a tree squirrel. If it lives underground or in rock piles, it’s probably a ground squirrel or a chipmunk.

Why Should I Identify My Tree Squirrel?



Non-native squirrels typically cause more problems than natives!

Knowing the difference matters.

It’s important to know what kind of squirrel you’re looking at. Some species can be managed with humane deterrents, but others are protected by law.





What’s Native—and What’s Not?

In California, there are now four established tree squirrel species, but only two are native. These two species evolved in this landscape and remain in balance with the ecosystems.

West Coast Native Tree Squirrels

  • Western Gray Squirrel (Sciurus griseus)
  • Douglas Squirrel (Tamiasciurus douglasii)

Non-native Squirrels - Introduced from Eastern US - early 1900's

The first documented release of Fox Squirrels in California occurred around 1904 at the Veterans’ Home in Sawtelle (West Los Angeles). Civil War and Spanish‑American War veterans brought them from the Midwest as pets and released them on the grounds. Since then, Fox Squirrels have become well established in Southern California and continue to expand into suburban and foothill landscapes.

These squirrels now dominate many urban and suburban areas. Although they were introduced by humans just over a hundred years ago, a brief period in ecological terms, their presence has begun to shift the ecological balance in unexpected ways.

They’re nearly as ecologically flexible as rats and are perfectly happy to nest close to humans and to eat the same native plants as western grays, plus human garbage and non-native plants that the westerns won’t touch. Bay Nature, May 2019

Squirrel Identification Matters

Accurate squirrel identification can help protect a species.

As of 2023, Western Gray Squirrels are now endangered in Washington due to habitat loss and population decline, with fewer than 1,500 remaining in the state. In Oregon, they're listed as a sensitive species, with populations declining in key oak-woodland regions.

Tree Squirrels vs. Ground Squirrels and Chipmunks

    

photos above: California Ground Squirrel and Sonoma Chipmunks





Ground Squirrel Species

The most commonly seen ground squirrel in California is the California Ground Squirrel (also known as the Beechey Ground Squirrel), which ranges across much of the state. However, California is home to at least 10 species of ground squirrels, many of which are tied to specific habitats, from alpine meadows to desert valleys. Some, such as the Mohave Ground Squirrel and the San Joaquin Antelope Ground Squirrel, are state-listed as threatened and face pressure from habitat loss.

Chipmunk Species

California hosts 13 distinct chipmunk species, all in the genus Neotamias. Unlike the California Ground Squirrel, chipmunks tend to have narrower geographic ranges and are often adapted to specific elevations or forest types. There is no single chipmunk species that is ubiquitous across the state. For example, the Sonoma Chipmunk is localized to the North Coast region, while others live exclusively in alpine zones or southern mountain ranges. None of these populations are currently considered at risk.

How Do Tree Squirrels Differ from Ground Squirrels and Chipmunks?

  • Nesting: Tree squirrels build nests in trees. Ground squirrels and chipmunks dig burrows underground.
  • Escape: Tree squirrels flee upward into trees. Ground squirrels and chipmunks retreat to burrows when alarmed.
  • Vocalization: Ground squirrels shriek in alarm. Tree squirrels scold after reaching safety. Chipmunks often give sharp, high-pitched “chip-chip” calls.
  • Cheek pouches: Ground squirrels and chipmunks both have cheek pouches for carrying food. Tree squirrels do not.
  • Tail: Tree squirrels have long, bushy tails. Ground squirrels have thinner tails, and chipmunks have short, flat tails with less plume.
  • Markings: Chipmunks are smaller and have distinct stripes on their backs and heads. Ground squirrels may have spots or stripes. Tree squirrels have neither.




What About Flying Squirrels?

Flying Squirrels are Not Tree Squirrels

In fact, marmots are more closely related to tree squirrels than flying squirrels are!

  • Subfamily: Flying squirrels belong to Pteromyinae
  • Activity Cycle: Nocturnal—active at night, unlike diurnal tree squirrels
  • Locomotion: Gliders that steer in the air with their flat tail. On the ground, their movement is awkward and limited—they rely on trees to travel and escape danger. Their gliding ability comes from a skin membrane called the patagium, which stretches between their wrists and ankles.
  • Diet: Primarily fungi, lichens, tree sap, and occasionally insects
  • Predators: Owls, martens, raccoons, coyotes, and domestic cats
