Squirrel ID Guide

Your guide to knowing which squirrel you are looking at...

Native: 2 species / Introduced: 2 species

Breakdown by size:

Douglas = small
Western and Eastern Grays = medium
Fox Squirrel = largest

The Douglas and Fox squirrels are distinguished from the gray squirrels by color, and are much more distinct in size. The Western and Eastern Grays are the most difficult to tell apart at a glance, especially where their ranges overlap.

To make things easier, we’ve broken the page into two side-by-side comparisons: Western Gray vs. Eastern Gray, and Douglas vs. Fox.

Western Gray vs Eastern Gray

The most distinctive difference is color.

Western Gray squirrels are one solid steel gray tone
- from nose to tail —
no mottling or fur color variation

Both species have white bellies.

Eastern Gray squirrels, however, often have mottled multi-toned fur.

They can show brown patches on their face, shoulders, and sides. Some even have a reddish tone or, in the case of the melanistic variant, are entirely black.

Walking

The native and introduced species look remarkably similar in shape and size.

  • Both have similar lengths and body weights (typically 18–24 inches including the tail).
  • They share a similar gait and posture — especially from a distance.
  • Behaviorally, however, they differ: Western Grays are shy and stay in trees; Eastern Grays are bolder and often navigate fences and roofs.

Most People Don’t Know What a Western Gray Squirrel Looks Like

Misidentified squirrels aren’t just found on social media—they appear in scientific databases, stock photo sites, and wildlife blogs. Even images labeled with the scientific name, Sciurus griseus, are often showing the wrong species.

Quick Audit: What We Found

  • Adobe Stock: Only 5 out of 20 images were true Western Grays. The rest were Fox, Eastern, Douglas, or ground squirrels—some mislabeled Sciurus griseus.
  • Shutterstock: 23 out of 39 images labeled as “Western Gray Squirrel” were incorrect.
  • iStock: Just 3 of the first 20 images were correct. Many were vaguely labeled “gray squirrel,” while others were clearly misidentified and tagged Sciurus griseus.
  • Google Image Search (top 20): 4 were misidentified Eastern Grays.
  • anomalia.bio.com: 5 of 6 featured images labeled “Western Gray” were actually Eastern Grays.
  • Scientific & commercial websites: Sites like BecauseTees.com, CABI, and ScientificLib routinely show Fox or Eastern Grays while discussing Western Gray Squirrels.

Why It Matters

If we can’t reliably identify native squirrels—even in published “educational” content—how can we protect them?

Accurate ID isn’t trivia. It’s the foundation for reporting sightings, managing habitat, enforcing protections, and educating the public. If we mistake the invaders for the natives, we risk losing the real Western Gray.
