California Tree Squirrels: Native and Introduced Species

Western Gray Squirrel (Sciurus griseus)

Characteristics

  • Large, silvery-gray coat with a pure white belly
  • Long, full, bushy tail with frosted edges
  • Weighs 400–850 grams; larger than most native squirrels
  • Eats acorns, pine nuts, fungi, flowers, and berries
  • Lives in mature oak and pine forests with continuous canopy

Behaviors

  • Shy and solitary; avoids humans and urban areas
  • Active during the day; nests high in trees in dreys or cavities
  • Travels through treetops—rarely descends to the ground
  • Abandons habitat if tree canopy is fragmented
  • Rarely seen in parks or suburbs

Douglas Squirrel (Tamiasciurus douglasii)

Characteristics

  • Small and quick with reddish-brown or grayish fur
  • Bright orange belly and dark stripe along each side
  • Tufted ears in winter; weighs 150–300 grams
  • Eats conifer seeds, fungi, berries, and tree buds
  • Lives in conifer forests at mid to high elevations

Behaviors

  • Extremely vocal—known for sharp, chattering calls
  • Highly territorial and alert
  • Diurnal and very fast-moving
  • Nests in tree cavities or builds dense dreys
  • Rarely seen outside of wilderness and national forests

Fox Squirrel (Sciurus niger)

Characteristics

  • Large-bodied with rusty orange underparts
  • Coarse grayish-brown fur on back; tail edged with red
  • Weighs 500–1000 grams—often larger than Western Gray
  • Eats acorns, fruit, seeds, bird eggs, and human food scraps
  • Common in urban parks, campuses, and neighborhoods

Behaviors

  • Bold and adaptable—often seen near people
  • Forages on the ground and in low trees
  • Nests in trees or in attics and sheds if available
  • Frequent visitor to bird feeders and trash cans
  • Introduced to California; outcompetes native species

Eastern Gray Squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis)

Characteristics

  • Medium-sized with gray fur and a white belly
  • Tail is fluffy and usually lacks reddish tones
  • Weighs 400–600 grams; slightly smaller than Fox Squirrel
  • Feeds on nuts, seeds, fruit, and cultivated crops
  • Found in urban and suburban areas with mature trees

Behaviors

  • Comfortable around people; often seen in city parks
  • Forages on the ground and nests in both trees and buildings
  • Highly adaptable and a strong competitor to native species
  • Introduced to California; spreads rapidly along riparian zones
  • May be confused with Western Gray but prefers human habitats
